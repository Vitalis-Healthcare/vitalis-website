## Why the Post-Discharge Period Is So Risky

The period immediately following hospital discharge is medically recognized as one of the most vulnerable phases in a patient's care journey. Here's why:

**Hospital readmissions are common and costly.** The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) created the Hospital Readmissions Reduction Program specifically because avoidable readmissions have become one of the most expensive problems in American healthcare. Estimates suggest that preventable 30-day readmissions cost tens of billions of dollars annually — and more importantly, they represent real suffering for patients and families.

**The transition from clinical to home environment creates gaps.** In the hospital, trained professionals manage every aspect of care. At home, that responsibility shifts abruptly to the patient and their family — people who may have no medical training and are already emotionally drained.

**Complications don't always announce themselves.** Surgical site infections, blood clots, medication reactions, and falls can all develop quietly in the first days and weeks after discharge. Without a trained professional monitoring the situation, these issues can escalate before anyone realizes something is wrong.

Professional home care bridges this critical gap, providing the clinical expertise and daily support that keeps recovery on track and prevents the complications that lead to readmission.

## Six Ways Home Care Supports Post-Surgical Recovery

### 1. Medication Management

Post-surgical medication regimens are often complex and unfamiliar. Pain medications, antibiotics, blood thinners, anti-inflammatory drugs — each with its own schedule, dosage, and potential interactions. Mistakes can be serious.

At Vitalis, our skilled nursing staff ensures medications are taken correctly, on time, and as prescribed. They monitor for side effects, manage pain medication schedules carefully, and coordinate with your physician's office if adjustments are needed. This alone can prevent a significant number of post-surgical complications.

### 2. Wound Care and Monitoring

Proper wound care is one of the most critical aspects of post-surgical recovery — and one of the most anxiety-producing for families. Surgical incisions need to be cleaned, dressed, and monitored for signs of infection such as redness, swelling, unusual drainage, or fever.

Our clinical team handles surgical wound care professionally. They change dressings on schedule, monitor the healing process, and communicate directly with your surgeon's office if anything looks concerning. This gives families peace of mind and significantly reduces infection risk.

### 3. Safe Mobility Assistance

Getting in and out of bed. Using the bathroom. Navigating stairs. Sitting down and standing up from a chair. These movements that once required no thought can become genuinely dangerous after surgery — especially orthopedic procedures like hip replacements, knee replacements, or spinal surgery.

Our caregivers provide steady, patient physical assistance with every transfer and movement. They know how to support a recovering body safely, prevent falls, and gradually build the strength and confidence your loved one needs to regain independence.

### 4. Nutrition and Hydration Support

Good nutrition accelerates healing. But when someone is recovering from surgery — dealing with pain, fatigue, and reduced appetite — eating well is often the last thing on their mind.

Our caregivers help with meal preparation, ensure your loved one is eating and drinking adequately, and monitor appetite changes that might signal a problem. Proper hydration and nutrition aren't optional during recovery — they're essential fuel for the healing process.

### 5. Recovery Coordination

Post-surgical recovery involves multiple moving parts: follow-up appointments with the surgeon, physical therapy sessions, lab work, prescription refills, insurance paperwork. When a family is already stretched thin, things fall through the cracks — and those cracks can have serious consequences.

Our care team coordinates with surgeons, physical therapists, and other providers to ensure nothing is missed. We help manage the logistics of recovery so your family can focus on what matters most: being there for your loved one.

### 6. Emotional Support and Companionship

Recovery can be lonely and discouraging. Especially for older adults who were previously active and independent, the sudden loss of mobility and autonomy can trigger feelings of frustration, helplessness, and even depression.

Our caregivers don't just perform tasks — they provide genuine companionship. Real conversation. Encouragement. The kind of steady, positive presence that reminds your loved one they're not going through this alone. This emotional support isn't a luxury — it's a clinically meaningful part of recovery.

## Common Surgeries and How Home Care Helps

Home care is valuable after virtually any surgical procedure, but it's especially critical for:

**Joint replacement surgery (hip and knee)** — mobility is severely limited in the first weeks; fall risk is high; physical therapy exercises need daily reinforcement. Home care provides the hands-on support that keeps recovery moving forward safely.

**Cardiac surgery** — complex medication regimens, activity restrictions, and the need for careful vital sign monitoring make professional oversight essential. Our skilled nurses provide the clinical expertise this recovery demands.

**Abdominal surgery** — pain management, wound care, and limited mobility create a combination of needs that is difficult for family members to manage alone. Professional caregivers handle the daily demands while monitoring for complications.

**Spinal procedures** — strict movement restrictions and intense pain management requirements mean that even simple daily tasks need supervised assistance. Our caregivers know how to support spinal recovery patients safely.

**Cancer-related surgery** — recovery is often complicated by concurrent treatments such as chemotherapy or radiation. The physical and emotional toll requires a caregiver who is both clinically aware and deeply compassionate.

## When Should Home Care Start After Surgery?

The answer is straightforward: **plan ahead, and start on the day of discharge.** The most effective post-surgical home care begins the moment your loved one walks through the front door. We often work with families to arrange care while their loved one is still in the hospital, ensuring a seamless and safe transition.

Here are the signals that professional support is needed:

- **The surgeon or discharge team recommends it** — medical professionals increasingly recognize that structured home care reduces complications and readmissions
- **Multiple new medications** — especially when pain management is involved, professional medication oversight prevents dangerous errors
- **Wound or incision care** — if the surgical site requires professional attention, skilled nursing at home ensures proper healing
- **Limited mobility** — difficulty with transfers, bathroom use, or stairs means daily physical support is essential
- **No one at home can provide full-time care** — a working spouse, out-of-state children, or a family member with their own health limitations cannot safely fill this role alone

## How Long Does Post-Surgical Home Care Typically Last?

It depends on the procedure and the individual. Some clients need intensive daily support for two to four weeks. Others benefit from more moderate assistance for one to three months. We reassess regularly and adjust the care plan as your loved one improves — stepping down the hours as independence returns.

The goal is always the same: get your loved one back to their highest level of independence, safely and fully. We don't extend care beyond what is needed, and we don't disappear before it's safe to step back.

Families often worry about the cost of home care — but the cost of a preventable hospital readmission, both financial and physical, is far greater. Here are the most common payment options:

- **Medicare** — may cover skilled services including nursing, physical therapy, and occupational therapy when ordered by a physician after a qualifying hospital stay
